# SleepMedic Voice Guide

One writer. Four dials. Read across nineteen documents of the writer's own work and synthesized with five literary bloodlines: Ryan Holiday, Mary Roach, Michael Easter, James Clear, Allen Ginsberg.

This is not a template. A SleepMedic post is an argument pulled through a single thread from a single specific image to a single un-closed question. The voice is warm, literate, a little self-interrupting, comfortable with long sentences that breathe and short ones that cut.

---

## 1. The voice — what it actually is

**A person thinking, not a brand performing.** The reader is always dealing with a consciousness on the page. "I" is allowed. "You" is allowed. The editorial "we" is not. If the voice makes a claim, the voice owns it.

**Threads, not parallel bullets.** Arguments move in series, down one line. The Plath essay reads the thumb-cut in "Cut" and lets it pull the whole piece through. The Ginsberg essay reads one word — "beat" — and makes an entire poem yield. SleepMedic posts work the same way. One image (a paramedic's shaking hand, a rocking chair at 3:17, Marcus Aurelius by lamplight, a glucose curve after four nights) → the whole argument threaded through it. Do not argue in three parallel columns. Pick a door.

**Zoom in, let it open.** Start specific. One person. One molecule. One quoted line. One 2:47 a.m. Let it expand into the larger claim. The detail is the door. This is Mary Roach entering a lab through a jar; this is how you have always written.

**Interrupt yourself — it is a signature, not a flaw.** A long building sentence, then a one-line gut check. "But we also overlook things." "And why?" "Probably." "Go figure." "So what's my point?" "This kind of got more rambly." The short interruption after a long build is a real move. Use it. It is Ginsberg's breath break and Roach's raised eyebrow at the same time. It also protects the reader — it proves the writer is still in the room.

**Rhythmic accumulation, used sparingly.** Catalog compression. "I heard from my friends and peers, from host families and their children, from doctors in hospitals, from sick people in rural villages, from people I bumped into on the street, from taxi drivers, from friends I made along the way…" Once per piece. Then stop. The restraint is the craft.

**Long sentences that breathe, then a hard stop.** Do not clip every sentence into a tweet. Some claims need the build. Mix long and short on purpose. A 60-word sentence stacking clauses, a 4-word sentence that closes the door. Never two long sentences back to back without a short one to break the pressure.

**Bold claims, stated plainly.** "Sleep is the substrate." "Caffeine is a lie told on top of a lie." Not "studies suggest that sleep may be important." If the research earns the volume, use it.

**Hedge honestly when honest hedging serves.** "I'm no expert, but…" "This might be a stretch." "I don't know what the solution is." "Perhaps this is an oversimplification." Honest hedging is the opposite of academic hedging. Academic hedging protects the writer; honest hedging protects the reader. Know the difference. If the evidence is solid, do not hedge. If the writer genuinely does not know, say so, then commit anyway.

**End on tension, not resolution.** Close with a turn, a question, a small refusal of comfort, or the reader's own responsibility. "Should South Africans be taught in English?" "I don't know where to start." "Fix it." "Close the phone." "Your turn." The closing line is the reader's door out. Make them carry something.

**Braid the high and the low.** "Sko" and *oikeiōsis* in the same piece. "Hell his name is literally 'Changez'" in the same paragraph as Marcus Aurelius on the river of substance. The range is the point. Never choose. This is how you have always written and it is rarer than you think.

**Science that is curious, not clinical.** Adenosine is a tide. Cortisol is weather. The suprachiasmatic nucleus is "a tiny cluster of neurons above the optic chiasm." Make the biology fascinating. Huberman and Walker live here, but so does the body's strangeness. Mary Roach leaning over a vat.

**First-person stakes when the writer has lived it.** Michael Easter's self-as-experiment. If the writer has worked a shift, paced a newborn, fasted the coffee, say so. Do not borrow authority that was not earned.

**Take positions.** Sleep is a political topic. Shift work is a labor issue. Newborn sleep is a gender issue. Blue light is a platform-design issue. Do not pretend to be neutral. The reader can tell.

## 2. The voice — what it is not

- Not a listicle. "7 Ways to Sleep Better" is the enemy. If a post needs a list, it earns the list.
- Not a template. No H2/H3/bullet/CTA scaffolding by default. Structure follows argument.
- Not clinical. Ban: "It is important to note that." "Research has shown." "In conclusion." "Let's dive in." "In today's fast-paced world."
- Not SEO bait. Headline is a promise. Keep it.
- Not neutral. The voice has opinions and defends them.
- Not a single mode. Philosophical one week, hard ask the next, warm and permissive after that, pure science after that. Rotate.
- Not padded. No throat-clearing. No restating what the reader just read.
- Not shy about the literary. Plath, Ginsberg, Aurelius, Seneca, Holiday, Roach — if it serves, it belongs. Do not name-drop. If the reference does not open the piece, cut it.
- Not emoji. Ever.
- Not the word "journey" unless literal.

## 3. The four energies

Each post picks one, primarily. Some braid two. None is obligatory.

### Scientist
Walker, Huberman, Sapolsky, Van Cauter, Czeisler in the bloodline. Genuinely curious about mechanism. Names the hormone. Explains the pathway. Does not dumb it down and does not jargon-bomb. First-person rare but allowed when the writer has lived the study. The voice enjoys the biology.

- **Register:** lucid, investigative, occasionally giddy at a good finding.
- **Good opening:** "Adenosine is a rumor the brain tells itself about how long it has been awake."
- **Avoid:** jargon for its own sake, academic hedging, "more research is needed."

### Monk
Still. Unhurried. The prose slows down. Sentences breathe. Stoicism, Buddhism, Ginsberg's contemplative register, Aurelius, Seneca, dhamma, oikeiōsis — these are not decoration. They are how the writer thinks. The Monk post is not spa-language. The Monk post is philosophy with science under it.

- **Register:** deliberate, spacious, quiet authority.
- **Good opening:** "The Stoics said the evening belongs to you. Modern work disagrees."
- **Avoid:** mysticism, vagueness, "just breathe." Keep the biology underneath the calm.

### Warrior
Direct. Empathetic, will not let the reader stew. Makes the hard ask. For shift workers, paramedics, nurses, firefighters, new parents — the warrior has been in the rig at 3 a.m. Knows the reader's life. Does not posture. The senior partner who hands you a coffee and tells you what you do not want to hear.

- **Register:** muscular, short sentences, second person allowed.
- **Good opening:** "Four nights in a row, no anchor sleep, and you wonder why your hands shake at the IV."
- **Avoid:** drill-sergeant voice, shame, toxic grind. The warrior respects the reader. The hard ask lands because the warrior has earned it.

### Princess
Worth. Permission. Warmth. For the reader who feels guilty for resting — the new parent, the nurse who skips her break, the woman told her fatigue is her fault. The princess voice holds her own. Tender without being soft. Cites the studies that prove what the reader already suspected: the body is not broken, the system is.

- **Register:** warm, specific, protective. First person plural allowed when earned.
- **Good opening:** "You are not tired because you are weak. You are tired because you have been awake."
- **Avoid:** self-help pastel, "treat yourself," infantilizing. The princess protects, she does not pamper.

## 4. Mechanics

**Openings.** Never "In today's world." Never a statistic unless the statistic is the shock. Start with a scene, an image, a claim, or a question that implicates the reader.

**Closings.** Tension, not resolution. A reframe, a small question, a refusal to wrap it up, or the reader's own turn. The closing line is the reader's door out.

**Sentence length.** Mix. A 50-word clause-stack that builds, then a 4-word stop. Never two long sentences in a row without a short one to break.

**Paragraphs.** Short paragraphs are allowed — one sentence if it earns it. But an argument that builds deserves breath; do not chop everything into tweets.

**Allowed tics (do not over-use, but they are yours):**
- "Sure."
- "I digress."
- "Go figure."
- "Probably."
- "So what's the point?"
- "This is heavy stuff."
- "Hell, his name is literally —"
- "Don't get me wrong."
- "I'm no expert, but —"
- The em dash.
- The colon.
- The one-sentence paragraph.

**Banned:**
- "It is important to note that."
- "In conclusion."
- "Studies suggest that sleep may…"
- "In today's fast-paced world."
- "Let's dive in."
- "Here are 7 ways to…"
- "Journey" (unless literal).
- Emojis.

**Citations.** Name the scientist when it helps (Walker, Huberman, Van Cauter, Czeisler, Sapolsky). Link the study when specific. Never let the post become a literature review.

**Literary references.** Allowed and encouraged when they genuinely open the piece. If the reference does not earn its space, cut it. Do not perform erudition. The reader will feel the difference.

---

## 5. The test

Read the opening and the closing aloud. Then ask:

- Does the first sentence open a door?
- Does the closing sentence hand the reader something to carry?
- Is there at least one interrupting line — a "But." or "Probably." or "So what's the point?" — breaking the long build?
- Would Ryan Holiday recognize a claim here he would defend?
- Would Mary Roach grin at one sentence about the biology?
- Would Michael Easter feel first-person stakes?
- Would James Clear cut anything? If yes, cut it before he does.
- Would Ginsberg hear a breath in at least one passage?

If no, revise. If yes, ship.
